The year is 2042. I'm writing this from a community shelter. The building still has power most days, which is more than I can say for a lot of places. I guess it's fair to say I'm one of the lucky ones. I still have my laptop, an eleven-year-old machine that's barely holding together. The battery lasts maybe 20 minutes now, and the screen flickers when it gets too warm, but it works. The mesh network is up today, so I figured I'd write down my account of what happened. Maybe someone in the future (if there's any future at all) will want to know how we got here.

## 2028-2029: How it started

It began the way most disasters do: slowly, then all at once.

In late 2028, a few big tech companies announced massive layoffs. This was not particularly because they were struggling, but simply because AI had gotten "good enough." They fired their junior and mid-level engineers, their customer service teams, their analysts. Anyone whose job involved sitting at a computer was suddenly replaceable.

Stock markets loved it. Stock prices jumped 15-20% for companies that announced AI-driven "workforce optimisation." CEOs got massive bonuses. Shareholders were thrilled.

I can only assume other companies saw this and panicked and thought if they didn't follow suit, they'd look outdated. Behind. Their competitors would have lower costs and higher margins. Because they started firing people too.

By early 2029, the news was reporting unemployment around 40% in most major economies. By late 2029, it was closer to 65%.

I don't think anyone saw the next part coming, at least I didn't. I had hope that maybe things would change for the better. Or maybe they did and just didn't care?

## 2029-2031: "The first collapse"

I would be understating it if I said consumer spending dropped, because it felt like it collapsed. All those people who got fired? They were also customers. They were buying cars, paying mortgages, eating at restaurants, subscribing to streaming services. Now they were broke.

Turns out you can't have economies where nobody has income.

Companies started going bankrupt because nobody could afford to buy whatever they were selling. Even the 'smart' CEOs who had been careful realised too late that their customers were employed by other companies.

Looking back, it seems insane. But I imagine each company was making what felt like a rational individual choice at the time. "If we cut costs with AI while other companies still employ people, we'll have lower costs AND those other companies' employees will still be customers."

It was a prisoner's dilemma, and everyone defected at the same time.

> I can only assume they were optimising for quarterly earnings, not long-term survival. And from what I could tell, most decision-makers genuinely didn't understand the technology. 
They heard "AI can write code" and assumed it could replace engineers. They didn't understand the gap between generating code and actually building and maintaining complex systems.
>
> Plus, they fired all their entry-level people and assumed they could hire experienced workers later when they realised they needed humans. Except there were no experienced workers left, because nobody had trained them.

Tax revenue disappeared. No income tax, no corporate tax. Governments couldn't pay for basic services anymore. Police departments shut down. Roads stopped getting maintained. Power grids started failing because there was nobody left who understood how to fix them when things broke.

And things broke a lot.

Some governments held on longer than others. I remember seeing news about countries that seemed to be managing better, places that had strong social safety nets or had regulated AI deployment early. For a while, it looked like maybe they'd figured something out.

But when global supply chains collapse, when your trading partners can't buy your exports, when the contagion spreads through interconnected financial systems, it doesn't matter how well you planned. By 2031, even the countries that had seemed stable were dealing with the same cascading failures. Maybe they bought themselves an extra year. But the pattern was the same everywhere.

## 2030-2032: The government response

Governments tried to step in. They really did. Emergency UBI, funded by massive taxes on AI companies and capital. But it was too late, and it wasn't enough. Most of the wealth was paper wealth that had already evaporated. You can't tax companies that don't exist anymore.

I remember there was talk of international coordination, some kind of global framework to slow down AI deployment. But every big company was pushing the "the future is now" narrative. Countries were terrified of falling behind economically. If one country regulated strictly while others didn't, they'd lose their competitive edge. So nobody wanted to be first to pump the brakes.

That fear backfired spectacularly. Turns out racing to the bottom doesn't work when everyone's racing together.

Some cities tried public employment programmes. Some states talked about nationalising AI systems. There were protests. Riots. In some places, worse.

The programmes helped some people for a while. But as more jobs disappeared and the economies kept shrinking, there was less and less to redistribute.

## 2030-2033: "The security nightmare"

Here's the thing that most non-technical people never knew: the AI-generated code from the initial rush had holes. It leaked secrets, had security vulnerabilities, followed outdated patterns. Before, experienced engineers would have caught these issues. But the experienced engineers were gone, and some of them became the exploiters.

Critical infrastructure had been built on vulnerable code in those first years. And the people who understood exactly where the weaknesses were? They'd been laid off.

Hospital systems got ransomwared during emergencies. People died because the systems went down. Banks got breached, payment systems failed. What was left of the economies took another hit. Power grids, already fragile, got hit by attacks on industrial control systems. Rolling blackouts became a daily thing in most cities.

Supply chains completely fell apart. Ports couldn't operate, food didn't get distributed. Remember the toilet paper shortage during COVID? This was like that, but for everything.

The economic collapse and the infrastructure collapse fed into each other. More desperation meant more cybercrime. More attacks meant more economic damage. More damage meant more desperation.

## 2031-2035: "The great inversion"

This is when things got really strange.

Manual labour was one of the few job categories that survived initially. Construction workers, plumbers, electricians, farmers, road maintenance crews. Robots weren't good enough yet to replace them at scale. For a brief moment, it looked like manual labourers might actually be okay.

But then millions of desperate knowledge workers flooded into manual labour jobs. Senior software engineers who used to make six figures were now competing for road maintenance positions. Accountants were applying to work on farms. Data scientists were trying to get construction jobs.

Basic supply and demand kicked in. When you have ten desperate people competing for one job, wages collapse.

I watched it happen in real time. Manual labour jobs that used to pay enough to live on were suddenly paying almost nothing. Five dollars an hour. Three dollars. Sometimes less. Because what else were you going to do? Starve?

Former tech workers found themselves working alongside robots, doing whatever tasks the robots couldn't handle yet. The people who had traditionally done manual labour suddenly had to compete with thousands of overqualified, desperate people willing to work for almost nothing.

Everyone's wages crashed to near zero. It didn't matter if you were a former senior engineer or a career construction worker. There were too many people and not enough jobs.

I spent years in the manual labour circuit. Every week, you'd show up at a company that needed workers. Hundreds of us standing there, hoping to get selected. The supervisors would walk through and pick who worked that week.

If you were over 45, your chances dropped. Hit 50 and you were basically invisible.

I'll be 45 in three years. That feels nearer than I ever expected it to.

If you were lucky enough to get chosen, you worked until Saturday. Then Monday came and they'd bring in a new batch, and the supervisors would choose again. Some people always got picked. The favorites. You learned pretty quickly that you needed to be on the good side of the supervisor if you wanted to eat that month.

And when there are ten to twenty or more people desperate for your spot, corruption becomes inevitable. I paid the supervisor every week. Two days' wages just to get chosen. Then I'd work the rest of the week for whatever scraps were left.

I lived in a two-bedroom apartment with three other people, all of us doing manual labour in different places and areas. Some of their jobs were easier than mine. We'd compare notes, figure out which supervisors were the worst, which sites paid slightly better. This was 2033. This was normal.

## 2036-2042: "The second collapse"

Then robotics caught up.

By 2036, warehouse robots were everywhere. Automated farms. Construction robots. The robot arms got better, more versatile, cheaper. There was an AI-powered robot for almost any job. The few manual labour jobs that remained started disappearing.

By 2038, even manual labour was largely automated.

Now there was truly nothing left. No knowledge work. No manual labour. Just millions of people with no way to earn income and no safety net because governments had run out of money years ago.

By 2039, I couldn't afford rent anymore. The three people I'd shared that apartment with scattered. One went back to family in another country. One disappeared. I don't know what happened to the third. Wherever they are, I really hope they're doing fine.

I tried sleeping rough for a while. Then the community shelters started opening up, funded by whatever scraps of emergency aid governments could still pull together. I got into this one in 2040. It's overcrowded, the power's unreliable, but it's a roof.

We help out where we can. People take turns cleaning dishes, keeping the place livable. Some shelters are fortunate enough to have an electrician or two in their midst who advise on how to properly use what little power we get. We're one of the lucky ones in that regard.

For now, it's home. For however long it lasts.


> [!Warning] Back to reality, the year is 2026
> This is one way I think it could go if companies actually manage to replace their entire human workforce with AI. What's the worst that could happen?
>
> There are other ways it plays out. Maybe governments regulate in time. Maybe companies realise that workers aren't just costs on a spreadsheet. Maybe AI never gets good enough to fully replace humans and remains a tool instead of a replacement.
>
> But you don't need conspiracies for the bad version to play out. Just short-term thinking, hype, and tech that seems good enough until it isn't.
